Kadri Temple

Home Story Location

The temple of Manjunatheshwara on the hills of Kadri is said to be built during the 10th or 11th century. It was converted to a complete stone structure during the 14th century.

The bronze idol of Lokeshwar (identified as Brahma) in the temple is considered one of the oldest in South Indian temples, dating back to the 10th century. It has an inscription dated 968 CE, mentioning King Kundavarma of the Alupa Dynasty. The temple also houses other significant bronze images.

Story of Kadri Temple

It is believed that Parashurama, after creating the coastal lands, prayed to Lord Shiva at Kadri. Lord Shiva appeared as Manjunatha with Goddess Parvathi, staying at Kadri for the betterment of the world.

This temple has Hindu and Buddhist histories. Buddhism was practised here till the 10th century CE. In spite of the decline of Buddhism elsewhere, devotion to the Buddhist figures of Manjusri and Avalokite≈õvara continued in this region. The Nath cult was partly oriented towards Buddhism as well as the Tantric Shiva tradition. As a result, many Buddhist temples were taken over by Hindu traditions. According to M. Govinda Pai, this temple was known as Kadri Manjunatha where 'Manjunatha' relates to Shiva and 'Kadri' is derived from Kadri Vihara, which was a Buddhist monastery of the Vajrayana cult.
